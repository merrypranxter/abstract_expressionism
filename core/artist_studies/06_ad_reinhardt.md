# Ad Reinhardt (1913–1967)

> "Art is art. Everything else is everything else." — Ad Reinhardt

---

## Biography

**Adolph Dietrich Friedrich Reinhardt** was born on December 24, 1913, in Buffalo, New York, to
German immigrant parents. His father, Frederick Reinhardt, was a laborer; his mother, Anna Heinz,
encouraged her son's intellectual and artistic development. The family moved to New York City when
Adolph was young, and he grew up in the city's intellectual climate.

### Education

Reinhardt was an exceptional student with wide-ranging intellectual curiosity. He studied **art
history** at **Columbia University** (BA, 1935) under the legendary Meyer Schapiro, the Marxist
art historian who was among the most influential art thinkers of the century. Schapiro's combination
of formal analysis with social history deeply shaped Reinhardt's intellectual framework.

He subsequently studied painting at the **American Artists School** and with Karl Anderson at the
**National Academy of Design**. His early work in the late 1930s was influenced by **Cubism** and
**Mondrian's geometric abstraction** — disciplined, rational, non-gestural from the beginning.

### The 1930s and 1940s: Geometric Abstraction

Unlike many of his Abstract Expressionist peers, Reinhardt moved from the beginning toward
reduction and clarity rather than expressionist gesture. His early geometric paintings — diamond
shapes, grids, hard-edged forms — put him in the orbit of American Abstract Artists (AAA), the
group of geometric abstractionists who were the other major tendency in American abstraction.

Reinhardt was also active as a political cartoonist and polemicist throughout his career,
contributing satirical art cartoons to *P.M.* (a left-liberal New York newspaper), *The Nation*,
and other publications. His cartoons about the art world — sharp, funny, politically aware — were
widely circulated.

### The Transition to Black Paintings (1950s–1960s)

Through the 1950s, Reinhardt moved through a series of increasingly reductive phases:
- **Brick paintings** (early 1950s): all-over grids of rectangular brushstrokes in subdued, close-
  valued colors
- **Blue paintings**: near-monochromatic canvases in ranges of blue
- **Red paintings**: similar approach in red ranges
- **Black paintings**: from approximately 1953–54, the ultimate reduction to near-monochromatic
  dark canvases

Each phase was a further reduction of the elements of painting, each a step toward what Reinhardt
called "the last paintings anyone can make."

### Teaching Career

Reinhardt was a committed and influential teacher, teaching at **Brooklyn College** (1947–1967)
and at the **California School of Arts and Crafts**, **Yale**, and elsewhere. His teaching introduced
multiple generations of students to both art history and the principles of his reductive aesthetic.

### Death

Ad Reinhardt died of a heart attack on August 30, 1967, in his New York studio, at age 53. He had
been actively making his Black Paintings until the end.

## The Black Paintings

### Description

Beginning around 1953–54 and continuing until his death, Reinhardt produced a series of paintings
that he considered his ultimate artistic statement. All are:

- **Exactly 5 × 5 feet** (60 × 60 inches) — every single one
- **Near-monochromatic**: Visually black, but containing subtle chromatic variations
- **Matte surface**: Painted with dry, carefully applied oil paint to eliminate any surface sheen
- **Cross-shaped structure**: The composition is based on a nine-part grid (3×3), with a central
  cross format embedded within the dark field — visible only after extended viewing in good light
- **Multiple layers**: Built up over months through many thin coats of dark paint

### The Subtle Tonal Variations

The key to the Black Paintings is the **near-invisibility of their chromatic content**. What appears
at first (and often for extended periods) as a uniformly black canvas reveals, under close attention
in good light, **slight variations in hue and value**:

- **Near-black red**: A very slightly warmer area, visible as minutely redder than its neighbors
- **Near-black blue**: A slightly cooler, bluer area
- **Near-black green**: A slightly yellowish-dark area

These variations are not labels or representations — they are barely-perceptible chromatic presences
that reward sustained attention. Reinhardt said they were visible only after "ten or fifteen minutes"
of viewing in proper conditions: a viewing commitment that most gallery visitors don't make, which
was itself part of his point.

The implied criticism: if you cannot see what is in the painting, perhaps you have not looked. The
invisible content demands the viewer's patience and commitment.

### The Nine-Part Grid

The structure of each Black Painting is a 3×3 grid, producing a central cross:

```
[corner] [top center] [corner]
[left]   [CENTER]     [right]
[corner] [bot center] [corner]
```

The cross is visible as a very slight value difference between the horizontal bar (slightly warmer),
the vertical bar (slightly cooler), and the corner quadrants (a third tonal relationship). This
structure is simultaneously present and invisible — you must look for it to find it.

The cross reference was deliberate but not, Reinhardt insisted, Christian. The cross was the most
neutral, bilateral, non-hierarchical compositional structure he could find — one that directed
attention to the center without creating a focal point, that balanced the picture plane without
imposing meaning.

## "The Last Paintings Anyone Can Make"

### The Concept

Reinhardt described his Black Paintings as "the last paintings anyone can make" — a claim that
provoked both admiration and irritation. What he meant:

All previous painting had relied on content — on imagery, on emotional expression, on formal
relationships between colors or shapes — as the justification for making marks on canvas.
Reinhardt had systematically eliminated each of these justifications:
- No imagery (pure abstraction)
- No emotional expression (the cool, neutral surface shows no gestural involvement)
- No formal color relationships (near-monochrome eliminates color contrast)
- No dynamic composition (the square format and symmetric cross are the most neutral possible)
- No evidence of the painter's hand (matte, smooth surface)
- No size variation (5×5, always)
- No change (he made essentially the same painting for the last 14 years of his life)

What remained? The painting itself — painting as such, freed from everything that is "not painting."
This was Reinhardt's radical reduction: art stripped to its pure self, nothing else admitted.

### The Art-as-Art Doctrine

This was systematized in his famous list of negations, variations of which appeared in many of his
writings:

*"No texture, no brushwork or calligraphy, no sketching or drawing, no forms, no design, no colors,
no light, no space, no time, no size or scale, no movement, no object, no subject, no matter. No
symbols, images or signs. Neither pleasure nor pain. No private or public. Nothing that is not of
the essence."*

The list reads like a Buddhist text — it is structured like the negative theology of the mystics
he was interested in, the apophatic tradition that approaches the divine by systematic negation of
everything that is not it.

## Meditation and Spirituality: Buddhist Influence

Reinhardt's deep interest in **Buddhist philosophy**, particularly Zen, was more than academic.
His engagement with Asian art and philosophy — he had studied Asian art history extensively — led
him to an appreciation of the Buddhist concept of "emptiness" (sunyata): not nothingness, but
the absence of fixed self-nature, the openness that makes all experience possible.

His Black Paintings aspired to something analogous to this emptiness: not void, but the clearing
of all accidental content to arrive at pure presence. The paintings demanded a quality of attention
similar to meditation — patient, non-grasping, content to be with what is without demanding more.

His relationship to Zen paralleled that of John Cage (music), Gary Snyder (poetry), and others in
his generation who found in Eastern thought a critique of Western art's obsession with ego-expression
and emotional gesture. Reinhardt's art-as-art doctrine was partly a response to what he saw as the
excessive ego-involvement of Action Painting.

## Art-as-Art Writings and Manifestos

Reinhardt was a prolific writer — essays, letters, manifestos, cartoons — whose writings constitute
one of the richest critical bodies in American art. Key texts:

### "Art as Art" (1962)
Published in *Art International*, Vol. VI, No. 10, December 1962. The most complete statement of
his mature position:

"The one work for a fine artist, the one painting, is the painting of the one-size canvas — the
single scheme, one formal device, one color-monochrome, one linear division in each direction, one
symmetry, one texture, one free-hand brushing, one rhythm, one working everything into one
dissolution and one indivisibility, one nontransparency and one luminosity, one direction, one
reflection — free, undisturbed from its own reflection, works into but not yet artwork, not yet
but not yet —..."

### Twelve Rules for a New Academy (1957)
A satirical but serious list of rules for "pure painting," including:
1. No texture
2. No brushwork
3. No sketching or drawing
4. No forms
5. No design
6. No colors
7. No light
8. No space
9. No time
10. No size or scale
11. No movement
12. No object, no subject, no matter

## Famous Works

### Abstract Painting No. 5, 1962
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 60 × 60 inches  
**Location**: Tate Gallery, London

One of the mature Black Paintings. In optimal conditions — good diffuse light, standing close,
allowing 10–15 minutes of sustained viewing — the embedded cross structure becomes gradually
visible, and slight variations in the near-black hue reveal themselves. The matte surface absorbs
light rather than reflecting it, giving the painting a presence different from either highly glazed
or conventionally matte surfaces.

### Abstract Painting, 1963
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 60 × 60 inches  
**Location**: Museum of Modern Art, New York

The title is maximally non-specific — "Abstract Painting" with a year, nothing more. The work
is one of the finest of the series: the tonal variations are particularly subtle, the cross
structure particularly integrated into the surrounding field. It has been called the culminating
work of the Western painting tradition's reduction of itself to essentials.

## Legacy

Reinhardt's influence has been enormous and paradoxical: a painter who insisted on art's autonomy
from everything else became essential to movements that seemed, on the surface, to be about
everything else:

- **Minimalism**: Donald Judd, Frank Stella, Sol LeWitt, Carl Andre all absorbed Reinhardt's
  systematic reduction and his interest in neutral, repeatable structures
- **Conceptual Art**: His insistence that the idea of the work was its most important element
  anticipated Conceptual Art's dematerialization of the art object
- **Post-Minimalism**: His process-based repetition (making essentially the same painting many
  times) anticipated the serialist strategies of Post-Minimalism
- **Institutional Critique**: His cartoons and polemical writings about the art market anticipated
  the institutional critique of the 1970s–80s

He remains simultaneously one of the movement's most extreme figures and one of its most
consequential thinkers.
