# Cy Twombly (1928–2011)

> "I work in waves; things develop in terms of themes, working back and forth until they're
> saturated... I work in waves of feeling." — Cy Twombly

---

## Biography

**Edwin Parker Twombly Jr.** was born on April 25, 1928, in Lexington, Virginia, a small city in
the Shenandoah Valley. He was the son of a former professional baseball player, Edwin Parker Twombly
Sr. (known as "Cy" Twombly, a nickname from pitcher Cyclone Young — which the son inherited), and
Mary Richardson. The family was Southern middle-class; Lexington was and is a conservative military
town (home to Virginia Military Institute and Washington & Lee University).

### Early Art Education

Twombly's early artistic formation was shaped by several key encounters:

- Study at the **School of the Museum of Fine Arts, Boston** (1948–1949)
- Study at **Washington and Lee University** in Lexington
- **Art Students League**, New York (1950–1951)
- Most importantly: **Black Mountain College** (summers 1951 and 1952), the experimental art school
  in North Carolina where he studied with **Franz Kline** and **Robert Motherwell**, and where he
  met and befriended **Robert Rauschenberg**

Black Mountain was a catalytic environment: the experimental, interdisciplinary atmosphere, the
presence of John Cage, Merce Cunningham, and Josef Albers, and the friendships formed there
(Rauschenberg remained a lifelong friend and early champion) gave Twombly access to the most
advanced American avant-garde thinking at a formative moment.

### The Influence of Military Night Exercises

A story about Twombly's visual development that has achieved legendary status: during his U.S.
Army service (1953–54), Twombly reportedly practiced drawing blindfolded or in the dark —
training himself to make marks without seeing what he was doing, developing a gestural automatism
that didn't depend on visual feedback. Whether apocryphal or true, this practice is consistent with
the quality of his mature work: marks that seem to come from somewhere below or beyond conscious
visual control.

### Move to Rome (1957)

In 1957, Twombly made the decisive choice that defined his mature life and art: he moved permanently
to **Rome**, Italy. He became deeply integrated into Italian intellectual and cultural life, married
a Roman noblewoman (Luisa Tatiana Franchetti, 1959), and immersed himself in the classical world.

This was not merely a romantic preference for the Mediterranean. Rome provided Twombly with:
- **Direct access to classical antiquity**: the ruins, the museums, the presence of two thousand
  years of accumulated Western civilization as a living environment
- **Distance from the New York art market**: freedom from the competitive, commercialized atmosphere
  of the American art world
- **A different relationship to time**: Roman time, in which the present coexists with the ancient,
  gave his work its characteristic temporal layering

He maintained studios in Rome, Gaeta, and Lexington, spending significant time in each.

### Late Career Recognition

Twombly's reception in America was complicated: his work was misunderstood, dismissed as childish
or empty throughout the 1960s–70s. European recognition — particularly in Germany and Italy — came
earlier and more completely. The definitive American retrospective at MoMA (1994, organized by
Kirk Varnedoe) reestablished his place in the canon and opened a period of wide critical and market
recognition.

He continued working until shortly before his death. He died on July 5, 2011, in Rome, at age 83.

## Gestural Scribbling: Marks Between Drawing and Writing

### The Character of Twombly's Marks

The most immediately distinctive element of Twombly's work is its **mark character**: what he
makes does not look like traditional painting marks. His marks resemble:
- **Handwriting** that has been abstracted beyond legibility
- **Children's drawings** of a sophistication children rarely achieve
- **Graffiti** found on Roman walls, accumulated over centuries
- **Automatic writing** in the Surrealist sense — marks produced before or beneath conscious control
- **Scratches** and **traces** rather than decisive statements

The marks are typically made with pencil, oil crayon (like Cray-Pas or similar), and paint applied
with fingers as often as with brush. They have an incompleteness, a tentativeness, a vulnerability
that is unusual and often unsettling in large-scale painting.

### The Painted Ground and Surface

Twombly typically worked on a **white or off-white painted ground** — usually a flat house paint
applied broadly to canvas or board. This ground has the quality of a chalkboard, a wall, a prepared
surface for writing rather than painting. The marks are made *against* this ground in a way that
emphasizes both their presence and the openness they leave around them.

### Vulnerability and Sophistication

The paradox of Twombly's marks is that they are simultaneously **child-like and immensely sophisticated**.
They appear simple, even clumsy, but they are anything but. They emerge from a lifetime of art-
historical knowledge, classical learning, poetic sensitivity, and technical mastery. The apparent
naivety is a form of sophistication — the achievement of someone who has learned enough to unlearn
the wrong lessons.

## Mythology and Classical References

Twombly's deep engagement with **Greco-Roman mythology** and **Mediterranean classical culture**
is one of his most distinctive characteristics. Unlike most Abstract Expressionists, who sought
to escape the burden of Western cultural history, Twombly immersed himself in it.

### The Mythological Subjects

Works from his middle and late career explicitly address mythological and heroic subjects:
- **Achilles** and the grief of Patroclus's death
- **Bacchus/Dionysus**: The god of wine, ecstasy, and creative dissolution
- **Leda and the Swan**: The violent myth of Zeus and Leda; sexuality and violation
- **Hero and Leander**: The tragic love story across the Hellespont
- **Orpheus**: The poet-musician who descends to the underworld
- **Apollo**: Sun god, patron of the arts; beauty and its limits

These subjects are not illustrated — there are no figures, no scenes. But the title activates the
mythological content; the viewer brings the story, and the gestural marks carry its emotional charge.
The painting and the myth interpenetrate without merging.

### Achilles and Patroclus

Among Twombly's most important series is his engagement with the **Iliad** — particularly the
friendship of Achilles and Patroclus and its tragic end. Several major works from the 1960s–1970s
address this subject, exploring the emotions of grief, rage, and loyalty through marks that seem
to be searching for, or already past, a loss.

## Text Integration: Poetry and the Visual Field

Twombly's integration of **text** into his paintings is another distinguishing feature. He
incorporates:

- **Fragments of poetry**: Lines from Keats ("Beauty is truth, truth beauty"), Virgil (Aeneid),
  Rilke ("Who if I cried out, would hear me..."), Sappho, Pablo Neruda
- **Names**: Of mythological figures, poets, places (Arcadia, Lesbos, Gaeta)
- **Numbers and dates**: Sometimes actual, sometimes invented, functioning as temporal markers
- **His own handwriting**: Not legible text but the gestural trace of handwriting as mark-making

This text is not caption or label — it is integrated into the painting's visual field, its scale
and placement part of the composition. The words often half-dissolve into pure mark; the mark
often crystallizes into near-legibility. The boundary between writing and drawing, between verbal
and visual meaning, is the productive tension Twombly exploited throughout his career.

## Mediterranean Influence and Rome

Rome gave Twombly something no other city could: a lived environment in which **the ancient and
modern were inseparable**. Roman walls accumulate centuries of graffiti, paint, plaster,
weathering; Roman streets juxtapose imperial ruins with baroque churches with 20th-century traffic.
This temporal layering — the sense that all periods coexist, that the past is not dead but merely
earlier — became Twombly's temporal and spatial aesthetic.

His Roman studio work reflects this: surfaces that seem to have been worked and re-worked over
extended time, marks that look old even when fresh, a quality of accumulated time within a single
painting. He was painting the way Rome looks — as if many different periods have left their
traces on the same surface.

## Late Color Works: The Bacchus Series

In his final decade, Twombly produced several series of paintings remarkable for their **dramatic
chromatic intensity** — a departure from the predominantly grayed, muted palette of his middle work.

### Bacchus (2006–2008)

The Bacchus series consists of large horizontal canvases on which **continuous loops of vivid
crimson red** swirl across the surface in great looping spirals. The loops are painted with large
brushes loaded with undiluted acrylic paint — fluid, generous, the marks accumulating and building
up in layers of red on red. The effect is visceral and overwhelming: a quality of Dionysian excess,
of blood and wine and creative frenzy.

The canvases are large — approximately 10 feet high by 20–30 feet wide in the largest versions —
and the red is uncompromising. Facing the Bacchus paintings is an almost physical experience;
the red is so saturated, so continuous, so large that it seems to envelop the viewer.

## Famous Works

### Cy Twombly (Nine Discourses on Commodus), 1963
**Medium**: Oil, pencil, crayon, and colored pencil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: Nine panels, each approximately 52 × 65 3/4 inches  
**Location**: Guggenheim Museum, Bilbao

One of Twombly's most complex achievements: nine large canvases forming a sequence that moves
through various states of visual intensity. Named for the Roman Emperor Commodus, known for his
cruelty and megalomania, the series uses grays, blacks, and flesh tones with pencil and crayon
marks to evoke a kind of historical violence — not illustrated, but present.

### Leda and the Swan, 1962
**Medium**: Oil, pencil, and crayon on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 75 × 97 inches  
**Location**: Museum of Modern Art, New York (1962 version)

The myth of Zeus transforming into a swan and violating Leda — a subject that has fascinated
Western art from ancient Greece through Michelangelo, da Vinci, and beyond. Twombly's version
uses pencil scrawl, paint smears, and fragmentary marks to evoke the mythological violence without
depicting it. The word "LEDA" appears in the painting in Twombly's characteristic scrawl.

### Bacchus (2006–2008)
**Medium**: Acrylic on canvas  
**Dimensions**: Series of large-format paintings  
**Location**: Gagosian Gallery collections; private collections

As described above: a late series of explosive crimson loops on large canvases. Among the most
powerful and surprising works of Twombly's late career, demonstrating that the artist who had
worked in grays and muted tones for decades was capable of a final eruption of color.

### Hero and Leander (1984–85)
**Medium**: Oil, chalk, and crayon on canvas  
**Dimensions**: Four panels  
**Location**: Tate Gallery, London; Private collections

An elegiac response to the Greek myth of Hero, a priestess of Aphrodite, and Leander, who swam
the Hellespont nightly to visit her until one night he drowned in a storm. The four-part work
uses pale blue, green, and white in loose, atmospheric marks to evoke the Hellespont, the sea,
the drowning — tragedy expressed through color and gesture, the mythological narrative activated
by the title without being illustrated.
