# Mark Rothko (1903–1970)

> "I'm not an abstractionist. I'm not interested in the relationship of color or form or anything
> else. I'm interested only in expressing basic human emotions — tragedy, ecstasy, doom, and so on.
> And the fact that a lot of people break down and cry when confronted with my pictures shows that I
> can communicate those basic human emotions." — Mark Rothko, 1956

---

## Biography

**Marcus Rothkowitz** was born on September 25, 1903, in Dvinsk (now Daugavpils), in the Russian
Empire (present-day Latvia), the youngest of four children in a prosperous Jewish family. His father,
Jacob Rothkowitz, was a pharmacist who had received a secular education and was unusual for a Russian
Jew of his time in being free of religious orthodoxy — though he later became more observant.

### Emigration and Oregon

In 1913, when Marcus was nine, Jacob emigrated to Portland, Oregon — drawn by an established Jewish
community and economic opportunity. Marcus arrived with his mother and siblings in 1913, seven months
after his father. The transition from a Jewish community in Eastern Europe to the Pacific Northwest
of America was disorienting and formative. His father died of colon cancer in 1914, just months after
the family's reunion, leaving the family in reduced circumstances.

Rothko grew up in Portland, attending Lincoln High School, where he was an exceptional student,
particularly in languages and debate. He won a scholarship to **Yale University** in 1921, where he
studied liberal arts. Yale's social atmosphere — dominated by entrenched Protestantism and social
clubs from which Jews were largely excluded — gave Rothko an outsider's perspective that would
become characteristic of his adult self-presentation.

### New York: The Art Student

Rothko left Yale after two years without a degree and moved to New York in 1923, eventually deciding
to become a painter. He studied briefly at the **Art Students League** under Arshile Gorky and
**Max Weber**, himself a student of Matisse and Cézanne. Weber's influence — particularly his use
of color as an expressive, emotionally resonant force — left a permanent mark.

In 1929 Rothko began teaching art at the **Center Academy of the Brooklyn Jewish Center**, a position
he held for more than two decades. Teaching children freed him from economic pressure while he developed
his mature style — slowly, deliberately, through the 1930s and 1940s.

### The 1940s: Mythological Phase

Through the 1940s, Rothko moved through a series of phases influenced by Surrealism and Greek mythology.
His paintings from this period feature biomorphic, floating forms with quasi-figurative references —
Antigone, Tiresias, ritual sacrifice, aquatic life. These works share the Surrealist commitment to
unconscious imagery but move toward an increasingly simplified and non-referential visual language.

Key works of this transitional period: The Omen of the Eagle (1942), Slow Swirl at the Edge of the
Sea (1944), Hierarchical Birds (1944). In these paintings you can watch the representational imagery
dissolving, the forms becoming more abstract, the color beginning to take on its own weight.

His 1947 essay "The Romantics Were Prompted" articulated the transition: "We favor the simple
expression of the complex thought... the large shape because it has the impact of the unequivocal...
the elimination of all obstacles between the painter and the idea, and between the idea and the observer."

### The Multiforms (1948–1949)

By 1948, Rothko had arrived at the "Multiforms" — transitional paintings consisting of irregular
blotches of color that floated on the surface, not quite recognizable as anything. The figures had
dissolved; what remained were pure chromatic presences. By 1950, the Multiform had crystallized into
the signature format: two or three stacked, softly edged rectangles of color floating on a colored
ground, the image occupying nearly the full canvas.

### The Classic Paintings (1950–1968)

For nearly two decades, Rothko worked within this format with extraordinary focus and subtlety.
The format never became mechanical because the real content — color, scale, luminosity, edge quality,
layering — was infinitely variable. Each work was a fresh investigation.

In 1958 he was commissioned to paint murals for the **Four Seasons Restaurant** in the Seagram
Building, New York (designed by Mies van der Rohe, Phillip Johnson). He accepted with enthusiasm,
produced 30+ large canvases, but ultimately withdrew from the commission (1959), unable to reconcile
the paintings with the restaurant context. "I hope to paint something that will ruin the appetite
of every son-of-a-bitch who ever eats in that room," he reportedly said — but when the restaurant
opened, he felt it was not the right setting for the work. Many of these paintings ended up at the
Tate Gallery, London.

### The Rothko Chapel (1964–1971)

The culmination of Rothko's career was the **Rothko Chapel** in Houston, Texas, commissioned by
Dominique and John de Menil in 1964. A non-denominational meditation space, the chapel houses
14 large-format paintings — seven triptychs and individual panels — in a nearly monochromatic
dark palette of plum, maroon, and near-black.

The chapel was designed in close collaboration with Rothko, who worked with architects Howard Barnstone
and Eugene Aubry after parting with original architect Philip Johnson over disputes about lighting.
Rothko wanted specific, carefully controlled natural light filtered through an oculus in the roof.
He worked on the chapel paintings for four years, considering them the most important work of his life.

He did not live to see the chapel's dedication: it opened in February 1971, ten months after his death.

### Declining Health and Death

Rothko's later years were marked by severe health problems — an aortic aneurysm in 1968 left him
weakened and restricted from large-format work by his doctors, who forbade him to work on canvases
taller than 5 feet. He increasingly turned to works on paper. His marriage to Mary Alice "Mell"
Beistle, with whom he had two children, Kate and Christopher, deteriorated and they separated in 1969.

On February 25, 1970, Rothko's assistant Oliver Steindecker found the artist on the floor of his
studio, dead from a self-inflicted overdose of antidepressants and a severed artery. He was 66 years old.

## Color Field Approach

### Floating Rectangles

Rothko's signature format — two or three soft-edged rectangles stacked vertically on a colored
ground — seems simple but is among the most sophisticated compositional inventions in American art.
The rectangles are never geometric in the strict sense: their edges are soft, slightly irregular,
luminous rather than drawn. They float: separated from the edges of the canvas and from each other
by narrow gaps of colored ground, they seem to hover in space rather than rest on a surface.

The format creates a specific viewer experience: facing a large Rothko, the rectangles are at roughly
head height. Their scale — typically 6 to 8 feet tall — means the viewer cannot take in the whole
composition with a single fixed gaze. The eye must move, must experience the painting in time. The
rectangles seem to breathe, to pulsate gently, the edges oscillating between the two colors.

### Soft Edges and Luminosity

The defining quality of Rothko's rectangles is their **soft edge**: the transition from rectangle to
ground is not a line but a zone of gradual intermixing, achieved through a combination of layering
technique and deliberate blurring. This soft edge is what gives the works their luminous quality —
the colors seem to emit rather than reflect light.

The perception of light is partly optical illusion: the layered glazes create depth, and the eye
interprets layered depth as luminosity. Rothko also carefully calibrated the relative values of
rectangle and ground — lighter rectangles on a darker ground seem to advance, darker rectangles
on a lighter ground seem to recede.

### Scale as Emotional Instrument

Rothko was explicit about the relationship between scale and emotional effect. He typically worked
on paintings 6–8 feet tall and 4–6 feet wide. At this scale, the painting fills the viewer's
visual field at normal viewing distance (2–4 feet). This immersive scale was deliberate: it prevents
the detached, aesthetic contemplation of a smaller work and forces a more immediate, embodied response.

"The reason I paint large pictures... is precisely because I want to be very intimate and human.
To paint a small picture is to place yourself outside your experience, to look upon an experience
as a stereopticon view or with a reducing glass. However you paint the large picture, you are in it."

## Emotional Theory

Rothko's stated goals for his paintings were unusually explicit: he wanted to communicate specific
emotional states directly, without mediation or translation.

### The Triad: Tragedy, Ecstasy, Doom

In multiple statements, Rothko identified these three conditions as the emotional register he was
working in:

- **Tragedy**: Not theatrical tragedy, but the Greek tragic sense — the weight of fate, the
  knowledge of mortality, the beauty that exists only because it will end. The dark Chapel paintings
  are the purest expression of this.
- **Ecstasy**: The intense, often frightening joy of the mid-period works — the blazing orange and
  yellow canvases of 1954–58. Not pleasant happiness, but the borderline experience of ecstasy where
  joy and terror intermingle.
- **Doom**: A specifically modern condition — the sense of historical catastrophe, the post-Holocaust,
  post-atomic weight. Rothko, as a Jew who had grown up in Eastern Europe, was not speaking abstractly.

### The Crying Viewers

Rothko famously said that viewers who cried in front of his paintings were having the same experience
he had while painting them. He reported that many people wept, and he considered this confirmation
that the work was functioning — that the emotional transmission was succeeding. He also said he wanted
viewers to feel "the intimacy and human need" that had produced the work, "rather than focusing on
relationships of color or form."

This raises a genuine question in aesthetic theory: how can non-representational color and form
produce specific emotional states? Rothko's answer was essentially phenomenological: color, at
sufficient scale, with sufficient immersive power, bypasses intellectual mediation and acts directly
on the nervous system.

## Technique

### Multiple Thin Glazes

Rothko's technical method was the opposite of Pollock's direct, physical application. He built up
surfaces through **many thin, transparent layers** — sometimes 3 to 7 layers in a single rectangle —
each layer modifying the one below, building depth and luminosity.

Each layer was allowed to partially dry before the next was applied. The result is a surface that
seems to have interior depth — you look into a Rothko, not at it. Light penetrates the thin layers,
bounces off the ground below, and returns through the layers with a slightly different quality: this
is what produces the distinctive glow.

### Ground Preparation

Rothko's ground preparations evolved and have been studied extensively by conservators:

**Early technique**: Simple rabbit skin glue sizing on canvas to reduce absorption, followed by a
ground of either gesso or diluted paint.

**Classic period technique** (studied by conservators at the National Gallery):
1. **Rabbit skin glue** (RSG) as primary sizing, sealing the canvas weave
2. **Dry pigment in a binder** as the first layer — often an egg tempera-based layer with dry
   pigment, creating a matte, slightly absorbent ground
3. Multiple layers of diluted oil paint in the main colors
4. A binder mixture combining **egg (whole egg or yolk)**, **oil**, **dammar resin**, and
   occasionally dry pigment — an unusual combination that creates a semi-matte, slightly tacky surface

This complex, non-standard binder — egg, oil, and resin combined — has caused significant
conservation problems in some works, as the different drying rates and behaviors of the components
can lead to cracking and surface instability over decades.

### Layering Process (3–7 Layers)

A typical Rothko rectangle was built up as follows:

1. **Layer 1 (Stain)**: Highly diluted paint applied with a large brush across the rectangle area,
   soaking into the ground — establishes the basic hue
2. **Layer 2 (Tone)**: Slightly more opaque, refining the hue and beginning to build luminosity
3. **Layers 3–5 (Body)**: Medium-density applications, building the characteristic glow; edges
   softened with a broad dry brush
4. **Layer 6 (Veil)**: Very thin, semi-transparent glaze over the main body — can shift the hue
   slightly warm or cool
5. **Layer 7 (Edge treatment)**: Careful work on the boundary between rectangle and ground —
   the soft edge is created deliberately at this stage, using a dry brush or fingers to blend

The entire process could take days per rectangle; large works might require weeks of layering.

### Egg Tempera Ground

The use of egg in the ground and binder was unusual and personal to Rothko. Egg tempera has
a distinctive matte quality and sets to a semi-hard, slightly flexible film. Rothko may have
been attracted to its traditional associations (it was the dominant painting medium before oil)
and its specific optical properties — less yellowing than pure oil, a different depth quality.

## Famous Works

### Orange and Yellow, 1956
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 91 × 71 inches  
**Location**: Albright-Knox Art Gallery, Buffalo

One of the most radiant works from Rothko's orange period (roughly 1954–1958), Orange and Yellow
places a soft warm orange rectangle over a cadmium yellow ground with a narrower yellow-orange
rectangle below. The effect is almost combustible — the two warm colors create intense optical
vibration, the painting seeming to emanate heat and light. At its proper scale and in good light,
it is genuinely overwhelming.

### No. 61 (Rust and Blue), 1953
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 115 × 92 inches  
**Location**: Museum of Contemporary Art, Los Angeles

One of the great works from the transitional period when Rothko was moving toward the darker palette
that would culminate in the Chapel. A rust-red rectangle floats above a deep blue rectangle, both
against a slightly lighter blue-grey ground. The emotional register is cooler and more somber than
the orange works — meditative, autumnal, with a weight that anticipates the Chapel's near-darkness.

### The Seagram Murals (1958–1959)
**Medium**: Oil on canvas (series of approximately 30 works)  
**Dimensions**: Most 8–9 feet tall  
**Location**: Tate Gallery, London; Museum of Fine Arts, Houston; National Gallery of Art, Washington

Rothko's approach to the Seagram commission was unprecedented: he wanted to create paintings that
would create "a trap" for the viewer, an environment from which there was no escape except into the
paintings themselves. The palette shifted toward dark maroon, burgundy, and near-black — suggesting
doorways or windows, architectural thresholds, entrances to unknown spaces.

Several of the Seagram paintings were damaged in the 1994 Tate flood. The survivors at the Tate
constitute one of the most important permanent installations of the 20th century.

### The Rothko Chapel, Houston (1971)
**Medium**: 14 paintings, oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: Most approximately 15 × 11 feet  
**Location**: Rothko Chapel, 3900 Yupon Street, Houston, Texas

The Chapel is Rothko's undisputed masterpiece — the work to which his entire career led. Fourteen
large-format paintings, arranged in a specific sequence around the octagonal interior space, move
through a range within the darkest part of Rothko's palette: plum black, maroon, dark brown, near-
black with subtle tonal variations. The three triptychs face the north wall, east wall, and west wall;
five single panels complete the enclosure.

The works were designed to be viewed in natural light, which shifts through the day and with the
seasons, changing the paintings' apparent hue and luminosity. In morning light, the plum tones
are more visible; in afternoon light, they tend toward near-black.

Many visitors report that extended time in the chapel — the paintings are experienced as an
environment, not as discrete objects — produces states approaching meditation or religious experience,
regardless of the viewer's tradition. The chapel has hosted Buddhist monks, Jewish scholars, Muslim
prayer, and countless private grief rituals. It is simultaneously Rothko's most personal and most
universal work.

## Philosophical Writings

### "The Romantics Were Prompted" (1947)

Published in *Tiger's Eye* No. 2, December 1947, this brief essay is Rothko's clearest statement
of his transitional aesthetic. Key passage:

"The Romantics were prompted to seek exotic subjects and to travel to far off places. They failed
to realize that, though the transcendental must involve the strange and unfamiliar, not everything
strange or unfamiliar is transcendental. The unfriendliness of society to his activity is difficult
for the artist to accept. Yet this very hostility can act as a lever for true liberation. Freed from
a false sense of security and community, the artist can abandon his plastic bankbook, just as he has
abandoned other forms of security."

The essay maps Rothko's rejection of both Regionalism (false security of subject matter) and
Surrealism (the exotic as an end in itself), in favor of a direct address to the universal through
abstract means.

### "Statement on His Attitude in Painting" (1949)

"It is a widely accepted notion among painters that it does not matter what one paints as long as
it is well painted. This is the essence of academicism. There is no such thing as a good painting
about nothing. We assert that the subject is crucial and only that subject matter is valid which is
tragic and timeless."

## Tragic Arc

Rothko's biography follows a trajectory that his work seems, in retrospect, to have anticipated:
from the luminous warmth of the orange and red paintings (the years of critical recognition, 1954–58)
through the increasingly dark palette of the Seagram period, toward the near-black of the Chapel —
a visual journey that parallels his psychological descent.

His health deteriorated after the 1968 aneurysm; he was lonely, separated from his wife, drinking
heavily, taking antidepressants that were known to interact dangerously with alcohol. The paintings
he was making in 1969–70 — works on paper in deep maroon and black — are among the most beautiful
and most desolate things he ever made.

He died by suicide on February 25, 1970. The work produced in the final years, taken together with
the Chapel, constitutes one of art history's most harrowing and complete artistic statements about
mortality, loss, and the persistence of beauty in the face of death.
