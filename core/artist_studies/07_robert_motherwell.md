# Robert Motherwell (1915–1991)

> "Every intelligent painter carries the whole culture of modern painting in his head. It is his
> real subject, of which everything he paints is both an homage and a critique." — Robert Motherwell

---

## Biography

**Robert Burns Motherwell** was born on January 24, 1915, in Aberdeen, Washington, the son of a
bank president. The family was comfortable and intellectually engaged; Motherwell grew up reading
widely and pursuing multiple intellectual interests simultaneously. He was, from the beginning, the
most formally educated and bookishly intellectual of the Abstract Expressionist painters — a fact
that set him apart from and sometimes in tension with his peers, who were often suspicious of
too much theorizing.

### Education

Motherwell's academic path was unusual for an artist:
- **Stanford University**: BA in Philosophy (1937), with a particular focus on aesthetics and
  the philosophy of mind
- **Harvard University**: Graduate study in Philosophy (1937–38), working with Arthur Oncken
  Lovejoy and others
- **Columbia University**: Study with art historian Meyer Schapiro (1940–41), who introduced him
  to the Surrealist émigrés then arriving in New York

He was fluent in French, deeply read in European literature and philosophy, and had visited Europe
as a student. This background made him an exceptionally articulate interpreter of modernism — both
practitioner and theorist.

### The Surrealist Connection

Meyer Schapiro introduced Motherwell to **Roberto Matta** in 1941. Matta in turn introduced him
to the circle of French Surrealist émigrés — Breton, Ernst, Miró, Tanguy, and others. Motherwell's
French allowed him to participate fully in these conversations (unlike most of his American peers).
His engagement with Surrealism was deep and lasting: he edited the landmark anthology **"The Dada
Painters and Poets"** (Wittenborn & Schultz, 1951), a scholarly compilation that remains a standard
reference.

Through the early 1940s, Motherwell explored **automatism** directly with Matta, making automatic
drawings and paintings that showed his immediate technical mastery. His 1941 journey to Mexico with
Matta was formative — the trip produced some of his first mature paintings and confirmed his
commitment to painting over the alternative career paths open to him.

### First Exhibitions and Critical Recognition

Motherwell's first solo exhibition at the Art of This Century Gallery (1944) — Peggy Guggenheim's
venue — established him immediately as a significant figure. His combination of gestural authority
and intellectual sophistication made him the movement's most articulate spokesman.

He was central to the founding of **Subjects of the Artist School** (1948) with Rothko, Still,
Baziotes, and Newman — a teaching and discussion forum that was one of the crucibles of the movement's
intellectual self-definition.

### The Elegy Series (1948–1991)

In 1948 Motherwell made a small painting as an illustration for a poem by Harold Rosenberg —
*"At Five in the Afternoon,"* a title borrowed from García Lorca's lament for the death of the
bullfighter Ignacio Sánchez Mejías. The painting showed two black ovoid shapes between vertical
black bars on a white ground. Motherwell recognized in this image something essential and began
expanding it into a series that would occupy him for the rest of his life.

The **Elegies to the Spanish Republic** grew to more than 150 works by the time of Motherwell's
death — paintings, prints, and drawings in varying scales that return obsessively to the same
fundamental composition: large black ovoids between vertical bars, the whole set against a ground
of white, cream, or ochre.

### Personal Life

Motherwell was married four times. His relationship with fellow painter **Helen Frankenthaler**
(1958–1971) was the most artistically productive of his marriages; their household was a center
of the New York art world. After the divorce, he married the German photographer **Renate Ponsold**
in 1972, a relationship that lasted until his death.

### Death

Robert Motherwell died of a stroke on July 16, 1991, in Provincetown, Massachusetts, at age 76.
He had continued painting until late in his life, maintaining the energy and ambition of the Elegies
even as he explored other series in parallel.

## The Elegy to the Spanish Republic Series

### Subject and Motivation

The Elegies engage directly with a historical event: the **Spanish Civil War** (1936–1939) and
the defeat of the Spanish Republic by Franco's fascist forces. For Motherwell, born in 1915 and
politically conscious in the 1930s, the fall of the Republic — supported by much of the American
intellectual left — was a defining political tragedy. The Elegies are acts of mourning.

But they are not illustrations of the war or of any specific event. They mourn through form: the
heavy black shapes, the tension between the organic ovoids and the rigid bars, the contrast of
the dense black with the openness of the white ground — these create an abstract equivalent of
grief and loss without depicting either.

Motherwell identified the black and white as carrying emotional content: black as death, darkness,
the weight of history; white as light, possibility, the life that has been extinguished. The ovoids
caught between the verticals suggest bodies, organic forms, life imprisoned and ultimately crushed.

### The Composition

The basic compositional unit of the Elegies is:
- **Two or more black ovoids**: Rounded, slightly irregular, painted with loaded brushes in
  continuous organic strokes; they suggest fists, stones, testicles, hearts
- **Vertical black bars**: More regular, structural, flanking and constraining the ovoids
- **White/cream ground**: The light against which the dark forms register; active, not passive

The variations across 150+ works are endless but always within this fundamental structure:
- The number and size of ovoids
- The spacing and width of the bars
- The proportions of the canvas (from intimate to monumental)
- The consistency and texture of the blacks
- The introduction of color (ochre, red, brown) in some later works

### Scale

The Elegies range from small works (12 inches or less) to monumental paintings of 6 × 10 feet
or larger. The largest works — particularly Elegy to the Spanish Republic No. 108 (1965–1966)
at 82 × 114 inches — approach Newman's scale and achieve a similar quality of physical presence.

## Collage Integration: Dada Influence

Alongside the Elegies, Motherwell maintained a sustained engagement with **collage** throughout
his career. His collages — hundreds of them, beginning in the 1940s — combine fragments of paper
(newspaper, musical scores, French cigarette paper, letters), color swatches, and painted areas
in richly varied compositions.

The collage work connects Motherwell to the Dada tradition he had studied as a scholar: Schwitters'
Merz constructions, Ernst's collage novels, Picasso and Braque's Cubist collages. But Motherwell's
collages are different in character from all of these — they are emotionally warm where Dada was
cool, atmospheric where Cubism was analytical, meditative where Schwitters was obsessively
accumulative.

### The *Je t'aime* Series

A long series of collages and paintings titled **Je t'aime** (begun c. 1955) incorporates fragments
of the French words and letters, references to French Surrealist culture, personal imagery, and
abstract painterly elements. The series demonstrates Motherwell's unique capacity to combine the
material of Dada (cut paper, found text, newspaper) with the emotional directness of Abstract
Expressionism.

## Gestural Abstraction: Plastic Automatic

Motherwell coined the term **"Plastic Automatic"** to describe his working method — an attempt to
reconcile the Surrealist concept of automatism (pure unconscious process) with the formal demands
of visual coherence. Pure automatism, he argued, produced chaos; formal control produced dead
academic work. The plastic automatic was the productive middle ground: intuitive but structured,
spontaneous but responsive to the painting's own emerging logic.

His working method involved:
- Beginning with automatic drawing or broad gestural strokes to establish emotional direction
- Allowing the composition to develop organically, following what the painting seemed to want
- Intervening with deliberate decisions when the painting lost coherence
- Revising extensively — the Elegies especially went through many stages

This was not the pure automatism of Breton's manifesto — it was a disciplined practice in which
unconscious impulse and conscious revision were in continuous dialogue.

## Literary and Intellectual Background

Motherwell was the movement's most explicitly intellectual figure — the philosopher-painter who
could discuss Hegel, Mallarmé, Kierkegaard, and Baudelaire with the same fluency he brought to
formal problems of painting. This background influenced his work in specific ways:

- His engagement with **French Symbolist poetry** (Baudelaire, Mallarmé, Valéry) taught him to
  value **suggestion over statement**: the work should evoke, not depict; resonate, not illustrate
- His study of **phenomenology** (particularly Merleau-Ponty) gave him a sophisticated framework
  for thinking about the relationship between body, perception, and meaning
- His Marxist background (through Meyer Schapiro and the intellectual milieu of 1930s-40s New York)
  made him attentive to the social dimensions of art and to the politics of the art market

## Famous Works

### Elegy to the Spanish Republic No. 34, 1953–54
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 80 × 100 inches  
**Location**: Albright-Knox Art Gallery, Buffalo

One of the early major Elegies, establishing the canonical composition at monumental scale.
Three massive black ovoids are flanked and partially contained by vertical black bars; the ground
is an off-white to ochre, giving warmth to what is otherwise a somber work. The paint handling
is free and energetic — the ovoids were painted with loaded brushes in confident sweeping strokes.

### At Five in the Afternoon, 1949
**Medium**: Casein on composition board  
**Dimensions**: 15 × 20 inches  
**Location**: Private collection

The small painting that spawned the entire Elegy series. Made as an illustration for Harold
Rosenberg's poem inspired by García Lorca, it shows the compositional structure in embryo:
black rectangular shapes on white ground, the rhythm of dark weight and white light that Motherwell
would expand into a 40-year project.

### The Voyage, 1949
**Medium**: Oil and tempera on paper, mounted on board  
**Dimensions**: 48 × 94 inches  
**Location**: Museum of Modern Art, New York

Not an Elegy but an important independent work: large, horizontal, combining ochre, black, and
raw canvas tones in a free composition that suggests travel, water, distance. One of the works
that demonstrates the range of Motherwell's achievement beyond the Elegies.

### Open Series (from 1967)
**Medium**: Various  
**Dimensions**: Various

A late series in which Motherwell reduced painting to its most elemental: a large field of a
single color (often deep blue, grey, or ochre) with a simple rectangular contour drawn in charcoal
or paint near one edge — suggesting a window, a door, an opening. The Opens demonstrate that the
artist who created the densely charged Elegies was also capable of absolute compositional restraint.
