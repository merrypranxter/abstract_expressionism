# Jackson Pollock (1912–1956)

> "When I am in my painting, I'm not aware of what I'm doing. It is only after a sort of 'get
> acquainted' period that I see what I have been about. I have no fears about making changes,
> destroying the image, etc., because the painting has a life of its own. I try to let it
> come through." — Jackson Pollock, "My Painting," *Possibilities* No. 1, Winter 1947–48

---

## Biography

**Paul Jackson Pollock** was born on January 28, 1912, in Cody, Wyoming, the youngest of five sons
in a family of Scotch-Irish descent. His father, LeRoy Pollock, was a farmer and surveyor; his mother,
Stella May McClure, was a domineering and artistically ambitious woman who encouraged her sons to pursue
cultural activities even as the family moved repeatedly across the American West — Phoenix, Chico (CA),
Janesville (WI), Riverside (CA), and finally Los Angeles.

### Early Life and Education

Pollock's childhood was peripatetic and unstable. His father was largely absent — working as a surveyor
across the Southwest — and his mother's controlling personality created a psychologically fraught home.
His older brother Charles, already studying art in New York, became his most important early influence.
Charles sent him art books and materials, and Jackson enrolled at Manual Arts High School in Los Angeles,
where he was exposed to Theosophy (through teacher Frederick Schwankovsky) and the murals of Mexican
painters — seeds that would flower later.

In 1930, at 18, Pollock followed Charles to New York and enrolled at the **Art Students League** under
**Thomas Hart Benton**, the leading American Regionalist painter. Benton's influence was formative
in two contradictory ways: his technique — energetic, rhythmic, full-body approach to painting —
became Pollock's physical foundation, while his subject matter (American rural scenes, folk mythology)
was eventually entirely rejected. What Pollock kept from Benton was the **kinetic energy** and the
sense that painting was a full-body practice.

### The 1930s: Struggle and Influence

The Depression years were brutal for Pollock. He worked on the WPA Federal Art Project, struggled
severely with alcoholism (documented from his teenage years, possibly genetic), and underwent Jungian
psychoanalysis — first with Dr. Joseph Henderson (1939–40) and later with Dr. Violet de Laszlo
(1941–42). The Jungian analysis was pivotal: it introduced Pollock to the concept of the collective
unconscious, archetypal imagery, and the idea that automatic drawing could access psychological depths
unavailable to conscious control. He filled notebooks with automatic drawings during this period.

Critical influences in the 1930s–40s:
- **José Clemente Orozco**: The Mexican muralist's powerful, dark, physically imposing work at the New
  School showed Pollock that large scale and emotional extremity were possible.
- **David Alfaro Siqueiros**: Pollock attended Siqueiros's experimental workshop (1936) where he used
  industrial paints, spray guns, and poured/dripped enamel — the technical seeds of drip painting.
- **The Surrealists**: Exposure to Ernst's decalcomania and frottage, Matta's automatic biomorphic
  forms, Miró's fluid automatist shapes.
- **Navajo sand painting**: Pollock saw demonstrations by Navajo sand painters at the Museum of Natural
  History (1941) and was struck by the idea of working on a horizontal surface, moving around the image,
  creating from all sides without a fixed viewpoint.

### Lee Krasner and Springs, Long Island

In 1941 Pollock met **Lee Krasner**, herself a serious painter who became his most important creative
interlocutor, champion, and eventually wife (1945). Krasner introduced Pollock into the New York avant-
garde circle — Hans Hofmann's circle, the circle forming around Peggy Guggenheim. She recognized
Pollock's genius before almost anyone else and worked relentlessly to promote him.

In 1945, Pollock and Krasner moved to a farmhouse in **Springs, East Hampton, Long Island**, purchased
with a loan from Peggy Guggenheim. Pollock converted the barn into his studio. The space — large,
unheated, with a rough wood floor — became the site of his greatest work. The distance from Manhattan
and the agricultural landscape of eastern Long Island may have contributed to the expansive, unhurried
quality of the late drip paintings.

### The Drip Period (1947–1950)

The canonical drip paintings were made in a four-year burst of concentrated innovation. Pollock had
experimented with dripping and pouring since the late 1930s, but 1947 marks the moment he committed
fully to the technique as the basis of a complete working method.

Hans Namuth's photographs (1950) and film footage (1951) of Pollock working — circling the canvas on
the floor, dripping, pouring, hurling paint — became among the most iconic images in art history,
cementing the "action painter" mythology.

### Late Period and Death

After the 1950–51 black-and-white paintings — a radically simplified series that introduced figurative
elements back into his work — Pollock entered a severe creative block. He produced relatively little
between 1952 and his death. His alcoholism worsened; his marriage to Krasner deteriorated; he entered
into a troubled relationship with Ruth Kligman.

On August 11, 1956, driving drunk with Kligman and her friend Edith Metzger, Pollock crashed his
Oldsmobile convertible into trees near his Springs home. Metzger died instantly; Pollock was thrown
from the car and killed. He was 44 years old.

## Techniques

### Drip Painting Methodology

Pollock's drip technique was not a single method but a **family of related approaches** developed
and refined over several years. The common element was the **removal of the brush as a mediating
tool between painter and surface** — the paint was applied through motion, gravity, and momentum
rather than direct contact.

#### Working on the Floor

The most fundamental departure: Pollock placed his canvas on the floor rather than an easel or wall.
This had several consequences:
1. **360-degree access**: He could approach from any side, work from all four edges, move around and
   through the composition.
2. **Gravity as tool**: Dripping and pouring from above used gravity rather than fighting it.
3. **Bodily immersion**: He could step into and across the canvas, integrating his body with the work.
4. **Scale freedom**: The floor accommodated any size without the structural constraints of an easel.

"On the floor I am more at ease," Pollock said. "I feel nearer, more a part of the painting, since
this way I can walk around it, work from the four sides and literally be in the painting."

#### Application Tools

Pollock used several tools to apply paint:
- **Hardened brushes**: Brushes loaded with paint, lifted and moved to allow drips and streams
- **Sticks**: Wooden sticks (often from his studio) used like wands to drip and fling
- **Turkey basters / syringes**: For more controlled pours of thin paint
- **Direct pouring from cans**: For heavier, faster application
- **Palette knives**: For scraping and embedding sand, broken glass, nails, and other materials

The artist Lee Hall documented that Pollock would sometimes use his hands directly, embedding them
in the wet paint surface.

#### Paint Viscosity Management

The key technical challenge was managing paint viscosity to achieve desired effects:
- **Thin paint** (highly diluted with solvent): produced fine, wispy lines; maximum flow
- **Medium viscosity**: stable drips and pools; the most common working consistency
- **Thick paint**: embedded texture, impasto effects in combination with thin layers
- **Aluminum paint** (used in several works): different viscosity and reflective quality

Pollock adjusted viscosity throughout a single painting, creating the layered quality where different
application consistencies are visible.

#### The All-Over Composition

The most radical compositional innovation: the elimination of hierarchy. In traditional painting,
some areas are primary (foreground, figure, focal point) and others secondary. In Pollock's all-over
drip paintings, **every part of the canvas is equally active and equally important**. There is no
center, no edge that is merely background, no figure/ground distinction.

This was noticed immediately by critics. Greenberg called it "decentralized" composition. The painting
continues beyond its edges by implication — it is a section of an infinite field.

#### Layering and Time

The drip paintings are built up in layers over time — often many sessions, each leaving its own
distinct record. Examining the paintings closely reveals:
- **Earliest layers**: often darkest, broadly applied, defining the spatial field
- **Middle layers**: building density and complexity
- **Topmost layers**: final energy, often the thinnest and most refined marks
- **Embedded materials**: sand, broken glass, nails, pebbles appear at various depths

This layering makes the paintings **temporal objects**: they encode time within their surface.

## Philosophy

### The Unconscious and Automatic Expression

Pollock's philosophical framework, developed through Jungian analysis and exposure to Surrealism,
held that the unconscious was a more fundamental level of truth than conscious intention. The goal
was not to express what he *thought* or *felt* consciously, but to create conditions under which
something deeper — something archetypal, something belonging to all humans — could emerge.

"I work from the inside out, like nature... The painting has a life of its own. I try to let it
come through."

The key phrase is **"let it come through"** — a passive construction for what was physically an
extremely active practice. The paradox is intentional: maximum physical engagement combined with
maximum psychological openness.

### "Being In the Painting"

Pollock described his working method as "being in the painting" — a state of immersive presence
in which the distinction between painter and painted dissolved. The body moving around the canvas,
the paint responding to his motion, his motion responding to the paint: a feedback loop that
bypassed conscious decision-making.

This idea had affinities with Zen Buddhism (which was entering New York intellectual circles through
D.T. Suzuki's lectures at Columbia in the late 1940s–50s), with existentialist philosophy (the
authentic act), and with the Jungian idea of the unconscious as a creative force.

### Native American Influence

Pollock was deeply influenced by **Navajo and Pueblo sand painting** traditions:
- Working on horizontal ground surfaces
- Moving around the work from all sides
- The process as ritual, not merely production
- The use of materials directly from nature
- The acceptance that the work might be destroyed or modified (sand paintings are traditionally
  destroyed after completion)

Pollock spoke of this influence directly: "The Indians have the right idea. They get it on
the ground and walk around it." This was not appropriation in the casual sense — it was a serious
engagement with an alternative epistemology of making.

## Famous Works Analysis

### No. 5, 1948
**Medium**: Oil, enamel, and aluminum paint on fiberboard  
**Dimensions**: 96 × 48 inches (244 × 122 cm)  
**Location**: Formerly in David Geffen collection (sold for ~$140M in 2006)

Painted during the first full year of the drip method's deployment, No. 5 represents the technique
at an early moment of self-confidence. The surface is dense — a web of yellow, brown, white, and
silver marks over a dark field. The composition has an almost nest-like quality, with a concentrated
warm center of yellow and tan marks giving way to darker, more agitated edges. It was painted on
fiberboard rather than canvas, which gives the surface a different quality of absorption and texture.
The work was long considered one of Pollock's finest.

### Autumn Rhythm: Number 30, 1950
**Medium**: Enamel on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 8 ft 9 in × 17 ft 3 in (266.7 × 525.8 cm)  
**Location**: Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York

Perhaps the canonical Pollock: vast, horizontal, and possessed of an extraordinary sense of movement
and space. The palette is restrained — black, white, brown, teal — but the composition is breathtaking
in its complexity and spaciousness. Unlike the denser earlier drip paintings, Autumn Rhythm has an
airy quality; the ground (raw canvas) is partially visible, contributing to the sense of light and
breath. The title came later (not Pollock's), given by curator Alfred Barr. The painting captures the
sense of walking through fall leaves — not as representation, but as kinetic experience.

### Convergence: Number 10, 1952
**Medium**: Oil and enamel on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 7 ft 9 in × 12 ft 9 in (236.2 × 388.6 cm)  
**Location**: Albright-Knox Art Gallery, Buffalo

Convergence was painted in 1952, after the black-and-white paintings and during Pollock's creative
crisis. Its extraordinary chromatic density — yellow, red, blue, white, black — represents a return
to color after the monochrome period and a kind of summation. The composition is among his most complex
and agitated; forms "converge" from all sides toward a dense center. At its full scale, the painting
is physically overwhelming. When first shown, it was offered as part of a government-sponsored
"challenge" — a communist journal had offered $1000 to anyone who could paint "something better."

### Blue Poles: Number 11, 1952
**Medium**: Enamel, aluminum paint, oil, and glass on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 6 ft 11 in × 15 ft 11 in (210.8 × 485.3 cm)  
**Location**: National Gallery of Australia, Canberra

The painting that launched one of the great art world controversies: purchased by the Australian
government for $1.3 million AUD in 1973, at a time when that sum seemed extravagant for an American
abstract painting. Blue Poles is distinctive for its eight pale blue vertical poles (applied with
a 2×4 board at the end of the painting process), which impose a loose architectural rhythm on the
otherwise all-over composition. The inclusion of glass fragments in the surface gives it an unusual
sparkle. The title "Blue Poles" came from the Australian press and stuck.

### Lavender Mist: Number 1, 1950
**Medium**: Oil, enamel, and aluminum on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 7 ft 3 in × 9 ft 10 in (221 × 299.7 cm)  
**Location**: National Gallery of Art, Washington D.C.

One of the most atmospheric of Pollock's works, Lavender Mist achieves an almost impressionist
quality — not through any representational reference, but through the extraordinary density and
subtlety of its mark-making. The title was given by critic Clement Greenberg; Pollock's own title
was simply Number 1, 1950. The "lavender" quality is produced by the interaction of blue, pink,
black, white, and aluminum silver — a chromatic mist. The surface includes hand- and fingerprints
pressed directly into the wet paint.

## Color Tendencies

Pollock's palette shifted significantly across his career:

### Early Period (1938–1946)
Dominated by dark, earth-toned Jungian imagery: burnt sienna, raw umber, ochre, black, blood red.
Works from this period have an oppressive, subterranean quality.

### Drip Period (1947–1950)
More varied: blacks, whites, silvers (aluminum paint), earth tones, with strategic uses of yellow
and occasional bright colors. The aluminum paint — used in Galaxy, Reflection of the Big Dipper,
Number 26 — gives these works an unusual reflective quality that changes under different lighting.

### Typical Drip Period Palette
- **Black**: Duco enamel black, matte; the primary drawing medium
- **White**: Titanium or zinc white, for lightening and negative space
- **Silver/Aluminum**: Radiator paint; reflective, industrial
- **Sand/Buff**: Raw canvas tones, earth pigments
- **Umber/Sienna**: Warm earth tones from earlier period
- **Teal/Blue-Green**: Used sparingly in several major works
- **Yellow**: Cadmium or chrome yellow, often as accent

### Black and White Period (1951)
Deliberately monochromatic: black Duco enamel on raw, unprimed canvas. Figures emerge from the
surface — some of Pollock's most psychologically disturbing works.

## Physical Approach and Gesture

Pollock's working method was described by Hans Namuth's photographs and Pollock himself as
**dance-like**: the whole body involved, not just the arm. He moved around the canvas in
long, sweeping arcs, crouching and straightening, approaching and retreating. The motions included:

- **Wide arm sweeps**: generating long, arcing lines across the full canvas width
- **Wrist flicks**: rapid, controlled drips in concentrated areas
- **Overhead drips**: holding a dripping stick or brush at shoulder height, moving quickly
- **Splatter**: shaking or flicking the application tool for spray effects
- **Scraping**: using palette knife or stick to move pooled paint
- **Stamping**: pressing materials into wet surface

The result is that the paintings encode **movement at multiple scales**: large compositional
arcs, medium-scale swirls and loops, small-scale splatter and drips. This multi-scale structure
is what gives them their fractal quality.

## Materials

### Paints
- **Duco enamel**: A nitrocellulose lacquer-based paint originally developed for automobile finishes.
  Fast-drying, glossy, flows easily when thinned. Pollock's primary medium from ~1947.
- **Alkyd house paint**: Benjamin Moore and similar brands; slower drying, good flow
- **Oil paint**: Used throughout career; in drip period often mixed with Duco
- **Aluminum (metallic) paint**: Radiator paint; used for reflective silver effects
- **Gloss medium and varnish**: For additional flow and glossy surface quality

### Canvas and Ground
- Raw, unprimed cotton duck canvas: most common, especially from 1947
- Masonite / fiberboard: used for some works (No. 5, 1948)
- Canvas stapled or tacked to the floor; sometimes laid on grass outdoors (several works)
- No traditional gesso ground in the drip period — the raw weave absorbs paint directly

### Embedded Materials
Several major works include non-paint materials embedded in the surface:
- Sand (Full Fathom Five, Lavender Mist)
- Broken glass (Blue Poles)
- Nails and tacks (Full Fathom Five)
- Combs, keys, paint tube caps (Full Fathom Five)
- Cigarette butts (documented in Full Fathom Five)
- Handprints (Lavender Mist, One: Number 31)

## Fractal Dimension of Drip Paintings

One of the most remarkable discoveries about Pollock's paintings came from physicist **Richard Taylor**
at the University of Oregon, who in 1999 published research demonstrating that Pollock's drip
paintings exhibit **fractal structure** across multiple scales — that is, they show statistically
self-similar patterns at different levels of magnification.

Taylor measured the **fractal dimension (D)** of the drip patterns using box-counting analysis:

| Period | Year Range | Fractal Dimension (D) |
|--------|------------|----------------------|
| Early drip | 1943–1945 | 1.10 – 1.25 |
| Transitional | 1945–1947 | 1.25 – 1.45 |
| Mature drip | 1948–1950 | 1.45 – 1.72 |
| Late period | 1951–1953 | 1.55 – 1.89 |

For reference: a straight line has D=1, a completely filled plane has D=2, and coastlines typically
have D≈1.25. Pollock's mature drip paintings fall in the range D≈1.45–1.72.

Significantly, Taylor found that human viewers rate paintings with D≈1.3–1.5 as most aesthetically
pleasing — consistent with the fractal dimension of natural scenes (forests, clouds, coastlines).
Pollock's paintings effectively **simulate the statistical properties of natural visual environments**,
which may partly explain their visceral, calming-yet-energizing effect on viewers.

Taylor's research has been used to distinguish authentic Pollock works from forgeries — fractal
analysis has been proposed as an authentication tool, though its reliability is debated.

## Influence on Subsequent Movements

Pollock's influence on subsequent art is almost incalculably large:

**Immediate (1950s–60s)**:
- Color Field painters (Frankenthaler, Louis, Noland) took his all-over approach and surface
  orientation but eliminated the gestural mark
- Allan Kaprow, who wrote "The Legacy of Jackson Pollock" (1958), saw Pollock's work as proto-
  performance art and developed Happenings directly from this reading
- George Mathieu (France) and the Tachisme movement claimed Pollock as a predecessor

**Medium-term (1970s–80s)**:
- Neo-expressionism explicitly revived the large-scale gestural painting Pollock had pioneered
- Jean-Michel Basquiat's layered, scrawled surfaces owe something to Pollock's all-over approach
- Happenings → Performance Art → Body Art: all trace back partly to Pollock's identification
  of the painter's body as a primary instrument

**Long-term (1990s–present)**:
- Fractal geometry research connected Pollock to mathematical analysis of natural complexity
- Digital paint simulation programs model drip and flow behavior to produce Pollock-like effects
- Generative AI trained on large image datasets consistently identifies Pollock's visual language
  as one of the most distinctive and replicable
- Physics-based rendering in games and VFX uses fluid simulation techniques that parallel the
  actual physics of Pollock's drip method
