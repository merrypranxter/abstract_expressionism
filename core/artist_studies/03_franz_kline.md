# Franz Kline (1910–1962)

> "I paint the white as well as the black, and the white is just as important." — Franz Kline

---

## Biography

**Franz Josef Kline** was born on May 23, 1910, in Wilkes-Barre, Pennsylvania, a coal mining and
industrial city in the Wyoming Valley. His father, Anthony Kline, was a saloon keeper who died when
Franz was seven years old. His mother subsequently remarried, and Kline was raised partly by the
family and partly in the Girard College in Philadelphia, a boarding school for orphaned or fatherless boys.

### Early Education

Kline studied at **Girard College** (Philadelphia) and then at **Boston University**, where he took
art classes. He spent four years in England (1935–1938), studying at **Heatherley's School of Fine Art**
in London, where he received a thorough academic training in drawing and painting — he was a skilled
draftsman and figurative painter long before his abstract breakthrough.

In London he met Elizabeth Parsons, whom he married in 1938. The couple returned to the United States
and settled in New York, where Kline worked through the late 1930s and 1940s as a figurative painter
of considerable skill — portraits, urban scenes, figures in bars and streets. He was known in Greenwich
Village as a brilliant draftsman, a witty conversationalist, and a regular at the Cedar Tavern.

### New York: The Cedar Tavern Circle

Through the late 1940s, Kline was embedded in the New York avant-garde's social world without yet
having made his abstract breakthrough. He was close to Willem de Kooning (their friendship was
central to both careers) and to many others in the Cedar Tavern circle. His figurative work was
appreciated but did not generate the excitement of his peers' more radical abstraction.

His wife Elizabeth suffered a serious psychological breakdown and was institutionalized for much of
the rest of her life — a burden that Kline bore with loyalty and that influenced the brooding,
monumental quality of his mature work.

### The Enlarger Revelation (c. 1949–1950)

The canonical account of Kline's abstract breakthrough involves **de Kooning's projector**: around
1949–1950, Kline used a Bell-Opticon projector to enlarge his small ink-on-telephone-book-page
sketches to wall scale. When the small brush marks were projected at large scale, they became
architectural — no longer decorative marks but structural presences, evoking bridges, girders,
industrial scaffolding.

This revelation — that scale transforms quality, that what is gestural at small scale becomes
structural at large scale — is the conceptual foundation of his entire mature work. He moved
immediately and confidently from small sketches to large canvases, and his first major abstract
works were exhibited at the Egan Gallery in 1950 to immediate critical recognition.

### Mature Career (1950–1962)

The twelve years from his abstract breakthrough to his death were extraordinarily productive. Kline
developed rapidly: from the early works' relatively simple compositions, he moved toward increasingly
complex, tense spatial arrangements in which the black marks and white grounds were in genuine
dynamic competition.

He became one of the most exhibited and collected American artists of his generation. Retrospectives
in Europe and the U.S. confirmed his international standing. He began introducing color into his
work in the late 1950s — first cautiously, then with more confidence.

### Death

Franz Kline died of rheumatic heart disease on May 13, 1962, in New York City, at age 51. He did
not live to see the full international recognition of the movement he had helped create.

## Gestural Abstraction: Bold Black Marks on White

Kline's mature work is instantly recognizable: **massive black marks on white grounds**, painted
at a scale that transforms gesture into architecture. But this description, though accurate, misses
the essential complexity.

### The Marks as Structure

Kline's marks are not decorative brushwork at large scale. They function architecturally: like the
steel I-beams they often evoke, they bear visual weight, span distances, create tension between
compression and extension. The black marks don't simply occupy space — they define and activate the
white space around them.

In Mahoning (1956), the black marks create a framework — a visual scaffolding — within which the
white areas become active, spatial, charged. The white is not background; it is the other half of
the painting. Kline was adamant about this: "The white areas are not just left over — they're just
as important as the black marks."

### Scale and Physical Engagement

Kline's canvases are typically 6–8 feet in their largest dimension, sometimes larger. At this scale,
the marks can only be made with a fully extended arm, with the whole upper body engaged. The largest
marks — 3–4 feet wide — require lateral movement of the entire body, a physical commitment comparable
to Pollock's.

The paintings have a quality of physical inevitability: each mark seems like the only possible mark
at that location. This is partly an illusion — Kline worked intensively from sketches — but it
reflects the real quality of decisive, committed action.

## Urban Influence: Steel, Bridges, Industrial Pennsylvania

Kline's visual language draws deeply on **industrial Pennsylvania** and **New York City's built
environment**. Growing up in the coal and steel country of the Wyoming Valley, Kline was surrounded
by industrial infrastructure: mine tipples, railroad trestles, factory buildings, iron bridges.
These forms — massive, black, structural, set against grey Pennsylvania sky — became internalized
as visual archetypes.

New York extended this vocabulary: the steel skeleton of buildings under construction, the bridges
of the East River (especially the Hell Gate Bridge, the Williamsburg, the elevated tracks of the
Third Avenue El), the girder-and-rivet language of industrial modernity.

Kline's painting titles reinforce these connections: Mahoning (a county in northeastern Ohio, named
for a Native American word, but more importantly the location of steel mills); Chief (named after a
locomotive); Painting Number 2, Accent Grave, New York (which could be a street corner or a
structural diagram).

But to reduce Kline to "industrial imagery" is reductive. His marks transcend their sources. They
partake of something more universal: the experience of force, structure, tension — the visual
grammar of things that hold up under pressure.

## Technique

### Commercial Brushes

Kline used **large commercial house-painting brushes** — 3 to 6 inches wide — rather than artist's
brushes. These wide brushes, loaded with paint, produce marks that have inherent variation: thick
in the center, thinner at the edges, with visible bristle texture within the mark. They cannot
produce mechanical, uniform lines — they are human instruments that record the pressure and angle
of the hand.

The choice of commercial rather than fine-art brushes was deliberate and significant: it aligned
Kline with the industrial materials of his visual world and produced a different quality of mark
than fine-art brushes could achieve.

### House Paint and White Enamel Ground

Like Pollock, Kline often used **house paint or commercial enamel** rather than artist's oil paint.
Alkyd-based house paint flows differently than oil paint, dries faster, and produces a more
industrial-looking surface. The glossy enamel blacks have a different quality than matte oil blacks —
harder, more definitive, more industrial.

The white ground was typically a coat of **white enamel** — either applied directly to canvas or
built up over a gesso ground. The choice of a bright, somewhat glossy white rather than the
off-whites of linen or the warmer whites of traditional grounds contributes to the starkness of
the black/white contrast.

### Newspaper Sketches and Projector Enlargement

Kline's working process had two phases: **small-scale sketching** and **large-scale execution**.
He made hundreds of small ink drawings — on newspaper, phone book pages, any available paper —
testing compositional ideas. These were not preparatory drawings in the traditional sense; they
were rapid explorations of spatial and structural possibilities.

The compositional ideas from successful sketches were then scaled up — either by working from the
sketch directly (holding it in mind while working large) or, in the early period, through projector
enlargement. The enlargement process was transformative: what worked at small scale didn't always
work large, and what worked large couldn't always have been predicted from the small sketch.

### The Working Process

1. **Canvas preparation**: White enamel ground applied over canvas, typically fairly thick
2. **Rough placement**: Often a loose gesture to establish the basic compositional thrust
3. **Black marks**: Applied with loaded commercial brushes in decisive, rapid strokes
4. **White corrections**: White paint used to modify the black marks — clarifying edges,
   reducing scale, adjusting negative space
5. **Back and forth**: The painting was worked from both colors; the white "erased" and
   revised the black, creating the painting's final balance

This back-and-forth process means that what looks like decisive, final action is often the
product of many revisions.

## Famous Works

### Mahoning, 1956
**Medium**: Oil and paper collage on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 80 × 100 inches  
**Location**: Whitney Museum of American Art, New York

Kline's most recognized work and a touchstone of mid-century American abstraction. Four heavy
black horizontal marks are traversed by two ascending diagonal marks, creating a structural
framework that simultaneously evokes a steel crane, a railroad crossing, and pure visual force.
The white areas are not uniform — slight variations in tone and texture give them depth. The
painting is from the year before Kline began introducing color, representing his black-and-white
approach at its most assured.

### Chief, 1950
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 58 1/4 × 73 3/4 inches  
**Location**: Museum of Modern Art, New York

One of Kline's first major abstract canvases, painted the year of his Egan Gallery debut. Chief
takes its name from a locomotive that Kline remembered from childhood. A massive horizontal black
mark — made with a wide brush in a single sweeping motion — anchors the composition; secondary
marks above and below create tension. The work is more direct and less complex than the mature
paintings but has extraordinary energy.

### Painting Number 2, 1954
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 80 7/8 × 68 inches  
**Location**: Museum of Modern Art, New York

The title is characteristically impersonal — Kline often resisted specific associations to let
the formal language speak. Three major black marks create a complex spatial arrangement: a broad
horizontal, a diagonal, and a vertical element that creates a juncture point of extraordinary
visual tension. The white areas range from open and airy to compressed and pressurized.

### Accent Grave, 1955
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 76 × 110 inches  
**Location**: Cleveland Museum of Art

The title refers to the typographical accent mark (`) — a small, precise notational device elevated
to monumental scale. The composition is organized around a single massive diagonal mark that sweeps
from upper left to lower right, with secondary marks defining the space above and below. The
painting demonstrates Kline's mastery of scale: the diagonal creates a sense of physical momentum
that is almost destabilizing.

## Scale and Movement: Marks as Architecture

A key concept in understanding Kline is the relationship between **mark, scale, and space**. At
the scale he worked, the marks become architectural. They create not images of things but structural
facts — visual load-bearing elements that hold the composition in tension.

The spatial tension in Kline is different from Pollock's all-over field. Where Pollock dissolves
figure/ground, Kline maintains it: the black marks are definitively figure, the white definitively
ground — but the ground fights back, pressing against the marks, making them earn their authority.

This tension — between the expansive force of the marks and the resistant force of the white ground —
is the true content of Kline's paintings. They embody structural stress, the experience of force
meeting resistance, weight meeting support: the engineering experience of the industrial world
translated into purely visual terms.

---

## Late Career: Introduction of Color

Beginning around 1958–1959, Kline began introducing **color** into his work — first cautiously
(muted greens, ochres, grays appearing in the "white" ground) and then more boldly. Works like
Shenandoah Wall (1961) and Sawyer (1959) use Prussian blue or deep red in place of pure black.
Orange and Black Wall (1959) introduces a warm orange field that dramatically changes the emotional
register.

The late color works are less celebrated than the black-and-white paintings, partly because Kline
died before fully developing the color direction, and partly because the stark black-and-white
became so identified with his achievement. But the color experiments suggest a painter who was
continuing to evolve and who might, given more time, have made his color paintings as significant
as his monochromes.
