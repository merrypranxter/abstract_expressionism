# Helen Frankenthaler (1928–2011)

> "A really good picture looks as if it's happened at once. It's an immediate image." — Helen Frankenthaler

---

## Biography

**Helen Frankenthaler** was born on December 28, 1928, in New York City, the youngest of three
daughters of Alfred Frankenthaler, a justice of the New York State Supreme Court, and Martha
(Lowenstein) Frankenthaler. She grew up in considerable privilege on the Upper East Side of
Manhattan, attending the Dalton School, where she studied painting under Rufino Tamayo — the
distinguished Mexican muralist who taught her that color could be an expressive force in its own right.

### Education and Bennington

Frankenthaler studied at **Dalton**, then at **Bennington College** in Vermont (1945–1949) — a
progressive liberal arts college where she worked with the artist Paul Feeley, who introduced her
to Cubism and to the possibilities of formal abstraction. She graduated in 1949 and returned to New
York, where she studied briefly with Hans Hofmann, the German-American painter whose ideas about
"push and pull" — the way colors advance and recede — would influence her thinking about spatial
relationships in flat color.

### Entry into the New York Art World

Through her relationship with critic Clement Greenberg (with whom she had a romantic relationship
in the early 1950s), Frankenthaler gained early access to the inner circle of New York abstraction.
Greenberg took her to Pollock's studio in 1950, where she saw the drip paintings being made. This
visit was formative: she was struck not just by the visual result but by **the working process** —
the horizontal canvas, the physical engagement, the way paint could be a liquid that moved freely
rather than a material pushed by a brush.

She also had access through Greenberg to the work of Cézanne, Matisse, Picasso, and Miró in depth —
the European modernist tradition that Greenberg was most engaged with. Her synthesis would be
distinctly American but thoroughly informed by European precedent.

### Mountains and Sea and the Breakthrough

On October 26, 1952, after a summer trip to Nova Scotia, Frankenthaler poured thinned oil paint
directly onto raw, unprimed canvas laid on her studio floor. The painting — **Mountains and Sea**
— was completed in a single afternoon session. She was 23 years old.

Nothing in American painting quite like it had existed before. The paint soaked into the raw canvas
like watercolor into paper, creating soft, atmospheric areas of color with luminous, stained quality
rather than the opaque, physical surface of oil painting on gessoed canvas. The shapes — loose,
gestural, suggesting landscapes without depicting them — floated in the cream-white of the unpainted
canvas.

Mountains and Sea is now considered one of the pivotal works of the 20th century, the founding
gesture of the Color Field movement's second generation.

### Marriage to Robert Motherwell

In 1958 Frankenthaler married **Robert Motherwell**, himself one of the central figures of Abstract
Expressionism. The marriage lasted until 1971. Their household was an intellectual and creative center
of the New York art world; their summer home in Provincetown brought together painters, critics,
and writers. The marriage gave Frankenthaler access to Motherwell's extraordinary library and
philosophical apparatus, while Motherwell benefited from Frankenthaler's more intuitive, process-
oriented approach.

### Late Career

After the marriage ended, Frankenthaler continued to produce large-format paintings — increasingly
using acrylic rather than oil, which allowed more control over the staining process. Her late career
involved exploration of printmaking (she made significant contributions to woodblock printing) and
continued development of the soak-stain approach.

She died on December 27, 2011, in Darien, Connecticut, the day before her 83rd birthday.

## Mountains and Sea: The Breakthrough (1952)

### What It Is

**Mountains and Sea** (1952) is a large painting on raw, unprimed canvas, approximately 7 × 9.75 feet.
Thinned oil paint — diluted with mineral spirits to the consistency of watercolor or ink — was poured
and spread across the canvas, which lay flat on the floor. Frankenthaler worked with a squeegee, a
sponge, and her hands, spreading and directing the pools of color.

The result has a quality unlike anything produced by traditional oil painting: the paint does not
sit on the surface but is **in the surface** — absorbed into the cotton weave, inseparable from it.
The colors are transparent and luminous. The cream of the unpainted canvas becomes an active part
of the composition — not ground but field, not absence but presence.

The title came after the fact, as was Frankenthaler's habit: she saw in the finished painting
a reference to the Nova Scotia landscapes she had just painted en plein air during her summer trip.
Loose blue-grey shapes suggest mountains; spread pink and rose suggest reflected sky or sea. But
these references are secondary — the painting's power is in its color and light, not its imagery.

### Why It Mattered

Mountains and Sea demonstrated several radical possibilities simultaneously:

1. **Paint as stain, not mark**: The traditional question "what kind of mark should I make?" was
   replaced by "how do I guide this flow of color?"
2. **The canvas as active agent**: The weave of the raw canvas absorbed the paint and became part
   of the color — a collaboration between painter and material
3. **Transparency without glazing**: Traditional transparency was achieved through layered glazes;
   Frankenthaler achieved it through absorption — a simpler, more immediate result
4. **Color freed from drawing**: No marks defined the shapes; the shapes were the color fields themselves

## Soak-Stain Technique

### Process

The soak-stain technique, as developed by Frankenthaler and refined through her career:

1. **Canvas preparation**: Raw, unprimed cotton duck canvas stretched on the floor (or lightly sized
   with rabbit skin glue in later works to control absorption)
2. **Paint dilution**: Oil paint thinned heavily with mineral spirits (turpentine or paint thinner)
   until it reaches the consistency of thin watercolor or ink. Ratio might be 1 part paint to 8–15
   parts solvent.
3. **Pouring and spreading**: Paint poured or ladled onto canvas, then guided with squeegees, broad
   brushes, sponges, or hands — or simply allowed to flow and pool naturally
4. **Direction and gravity**: Tilting the canvas, using gravity to direct flow; pools allowed to
   spread until they reach a natural resting position
5. **Masking the rest**: Areas not to be stained protected by covering or by working sequentially
6. **Layering**: Multiple pourings, allowing each to dry partially before adding the next

The key is **absorption**: the paint is not sitting on the surface but is chemically bound to the
canvas fiber. This is what distinguishes it from Pollock's poured paint (which sits on the surface of
his gessoed canvas) and produces the characteristic stained, luminous quality.

### Working with Transparency and Accident

Frankenthaler embraced the inherent unpredictability of the pour: paint moves toward the low point
of a tilted canvas, spreads along weave channels, pools differently on different canvas densities.
These accidents were not errors to be corrected but **information to be worked with**.

Her aesthetic criterion was whether the accidental had been integrated — whether the painting felt
"all of a piece," as she often said, or whether it showed signs of struggle. The best paintings feel
like they happened at once, even when they were built up over many sessions.

## Color Field Connection: Bridge Between Pollock and Color Field

Frankenthaler occupies a pivotal position in the history of abstract painting as the **bridge between
Action Painting and Color Field**. She took Pollock's horizontal canvas and his dissolution of
figure/ground and pushed them in a different direction: toward color as field, toward atmospheric
space, toward the elimination of gestural mark-making.

From Pollock she inherited:
- Working on the floor
- Pouring and gravity as compositional tools
- The all-over orientation
- The embrace of accident

From Cézanne and Matisse she inherited:
- Color as structural element
- The relationship between shape and space
- The importance of the edge as compositional moment

Her synthesis produced something neither Pollock nor the European colorists had achieved: **color
experienced as atmosphere** — not the flat chromatic fields of Matisse, not the energetic marks of
Pollock, but color that suffused and breathed like light through air.

## Flowing, Organic Forms

Frankenthaler's shapes are consistently **fluid, organic, and landscape-referent**, even in her
most abstract works. They recall the shapes produced by flowing water, accumulating clouds, eroded
rock, growing organisms — natural processes rather than geometric constructions or gestural decisions.

This organic quality reflects her fundamental aesthetic orientation: she was painting with and through
natural processes (gravity, absorption, flow) rather than imposing forms on a surface. The shapes
were discoveries, not intentions. They emerged from the interaction of diluted paint, raw canvas,
and her guidance — a collaboration between artist and material.

Even her most abstract paintings retain a sense of **landscape space**: a horizontal orientation,
areas that read as sky and earth, light coming from above.

## Influence on Morris Louis and Kenneth Noland

The direct influence of Frankenthaler on Morris Louis and Kenneth Noland is one of the best-
documented chains of influence in Abstract Expressionism.

In April 1953, **Clement Greenberg** brought Morris Louis and Kenneth Noland to Frankenthaler's
studio to see her recent work — specifically Mountains and Sea and related paintings. Both artists
were deeply affected. Louis later said it was "a bridge between Pollock and what was possible."

**Morris Louis** (1912–1962): Within months of the studio visit, Louis had adopted the soak-stain
technique and began the series of paintings — the Veils, the Unfurleds, the Stripes — that constitute
his major achievement. His technique involved pouring large quantities of Magna acrylic paint across
canvas, creating translucent veils of color that are among the most purely optical works in American
art. Louis gave Frankenthaler's technique its fullest possible development, purifying it to an almost
total elimination of the artist's gesture.

**Kenneth Noland** (1924–2010): Also adopted soak-stain and developed from it the highly geometric
targets (concentric circles), chevrons, and horizontal bands that became his signature. Noland took
the technique in a more geometric, systematic direction.

Both Louis and Noland are associated with the "Washington Color School" and with Greenberg's
formalist program — further evidence of Frankenthaler's centrality to the development of American
abstraction.

## Famous Works

### Mountains and Sea, 1952
**Medium**: Oil and charcoal on canvas (unprimed)  
**Dimensions**: 86 5/8 × 117 1/4 inches  
**Location**: National Gallery of Art, Washington D.C. (on loan from artist's collection)

The founding gesture of the soak-stain technique and one of the pivotal works in 20th-century
American painting. Described in detail above.

### Interior Landscape, 1964
**Medium**: Acrylic on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 104 3/4 × 92 5/8 inches  
**Location**: San Francisco Museum of Modern Art

A mature work from the period after Frankenthaler began using acrylic (Magna and later Liquitex)
rather than oil. The acrylic's faster drying time and different absorption quality required adjustment
of her technique. Interior Landscape demonstrates her mastery of the evolved method: large areas of
orange, pink, green, and deep blue pool and interact across the full canvas, the edges where colors
meet producing complex, soft transitions. The acrylic gives the colors a different, more saturated
quality than the oil works.

### Flood, 1967
**Medium**: Acrylic on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 124 × 140 inches  
**Location**: Whitney Museum of American Art

One of Frankenthaler's most powerful works: a vast horizontal canvas dominated by dark blue and
green flowing into pools, with areas of lighter color emerging at the edges. The title is perfectly
chosen — the painting captures the appearance and sensation of water rising, color spreading
unstoppably. The composition is nearly symmetrical but not quite, the slight asymmetry creating
tension.

### Buddha, 1983
**Medium**: Acrylic on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 72 × 126 inches  
**Location**: Private collection

A late work in which Frankenthaler's landscapes have achieved a quality of almost spiritual
simplicity. A calm, pale wash of light-suffused color fills most of the canvas; dark areas at the
edges and a few central forms create the composition. The title suggests the contemplative, centered
quality of the painting without imposing a narrative.
