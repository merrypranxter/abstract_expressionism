# Barnett Newman (1905–1970)

> "The impulse of modern art was this desire to destroy beauty... Instead of making cathedrals out
> of Christ, man, or 'life', we are making it out of ourselves, out of our own feelings. The image
> we produce is the self-evident one of revelation, real and concrete, that can be understood by
> anyone who will look at it without the nostalgic glasses of history." — Barnett Newman, 1948

---

## Biography

**Barnett Newman** was born on January 29, 1905, in New York City, to Ashkenazi Jewish immigrants
from Poland — Abraham Newman and Anna Perlmutter. He grew up in the Bronx in a culturally rich
Jewish immigrant household. His father owned a clothing factory and was something of a autodidact
intellectual; the family valued education and cultural engagement.

### Education and Early Ambitions

Newman studied at the **Art Students League** of New York (1922–26) while simultaneously pursuing
a degree in philosophy at **City College of New York** (BA, 1927). This dual education — practical
art training alongside systematic philosophy — was fundamental to his development. Unlike most of
his Abstract Expressionist peers, Newman was a genuine intellectual: he read widely in philosophy,
science, and literature and brought this reading to bear directly on his artistic thinking.

Throughout the 1930s, Newman's painting career was largely frustrated. He destroyed much of his
early work and made very few public exhibitions. He sustained himself partly through his father's
business and by teaching art in the New York public school system. During this period he wrote
extensively — essays, catalog introductions, art criticism — that established his intellectual
position within the emerging New York avant-garde.

### The 1940s: Development of a Philosophical Program

Through the 1940s, Newman developed the ideas that would underpin his mature work:
- A deep engagement with **Kabbalistic mysticism** and the concept of *tsimtsum* (the creative act
  of divine withdrawal that makes space for creation)
- The reading of **Edmund Burke's "A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the
  Sublime and Beautiful"** (1757), which made a fundamental distinction between beauty (pleasurable,
  ordered, finite) and the sublime (terrifying, boundless, overwhelming)
- His engagement with **Surrealist automatism** and the question of how unconscious impulses could
  be accessed through art
- His idea that **subject matter** — not purely formal relationships — was essential to serious art

### Onement I: The Revelation (January 29, 1948)

On his birthday in 1948, Newman painted **Onement I**: a small (27 × 16 inches) canvas of Indian
Red with a single vertical strip of cadmium orange tape in the center, over-painted with orange
paint applied with a palette knife. Newman reportedly pinned the painting to the wall of his studio
and stared at it for months before understanding what he had made.

What he recognized was that the vertical strip — which he would call the "zip" — was not a mark
in the conventional sense, not a drawn line, but a **threshold**: a place where space was divided
and created simultaneously. The zip didn't cross the field; it defined the field by establishing
its center, its edge, its scale. The painting was not *about* a zip any more than it was about the
red field — it *was* the encounter between the two presences.

From this revelation, all his subsequent work flowed.

### Mature Career (1950–1970)

Newman had his first solo show at the Betty Parsons Gallery in 1950 — to almost universal critical
hostility and incomprehension. His peers in the Abstract Expressionist circle, who valued the gestural
and the expressive, were generally baffled by his spare, apparently simple paintings. Even Greenberg,
his early champion, eventually distanced himself.

Recognition came slowly. The 1959 Documenta II in Kassel, Germany; a retrospective at the French
& Company gallery in New York (1959); the São Paulo Bienal (1965). By the late 1960s, as Minimalism
and Color Field painting had claimed him as a precursor, Newman was finally recognized as one of the
most consequential painters of the century.

He died of a heart attack on July 4, 1970, in New York City, at age 65.

## The "Zip" Paintings

### What the Zip Is

Newman's "zip" is a vertical element in his paintings — typically a narrow band of color, one to
several inches wide, running the full height of the canvas. This description makes it sound simple;
its function is extraordinarily complex.

The zip is:
- **Not a line**: It has measurable width; it is a band or strip, not a linear drawing
- **Not a divider**: Though it visually divides the field, it more accurately *creates* the field
  by establishing spatial relationships and scale
- **Not a figure**: The zip and the field are co-equal presences; neither is background to the other
- **A threshold**: Newman associated the zip with the divine creative act — the moment of separation
  that enables existence

In Newman's theological-aesthetic framework (drawing on Kabbalistic sources), the zip related to
the concept of *tsimtsum* — God's creative withdrawal to make space for the world. The zip is the
act of creation inscribed in the field: a moment of distinction that brings forth meaning from void.

### Scale Relationships

The zip's visual function depends critically on its relationship to the overall scale of the painting.
In a small painting, a zip reads as a formal element, a compositional device. In a very large painting —
at Newman's preferred scales — the zip becomes a **human-scale measure**: approximately the width
of a human figure, the scale of the body confronting the vast field.

Facing a very large Newman, the viewer's body is implicitly inscribed in the painting through the
zip's scale. The zip says: this is roughly you, and this is the immensity against which you stand.

## The Concept of the Sublime

Newman's 1948 essay **"The Sublime is Now"** is one of the most important theoretical texts of
the period. In it, Newman argued:

1. European art had been trapped between two inadequate aesthetic categories: **beauty** (Greek
   rationalism, formal harmony) and the **picturesque** (Romantic emotionalism)
2. The American artist, freed from this history, could attempt something neither European tradition
   had achieved: the direct expression of the **sublime** — an encounter with the absolute, the
   boundless, the overwhelming
3. The sublime, properly understood, requires not landscapes (as in European Romantic painting) but
   the confrontation with pure feeling: "Instead of making cathedrals out of Christ, man, or life,
   we are making it out of ourselves, out of our own feelings."

Newman's understanding of the sublime drew on Burke's original formulation (terror at the boundless)
but transformed it: the sublime of his paintings is not terror at infinity but the confrontation with
one's own existence in the face of that which exceeds comprehension.

### Terror and Beauty

Newman's sublime combines **terror** (the overwhelming scale, the impossibility of grasping the whole)
with **beauty** (the luminous color, the precision of the zip, the visual pleasure of the field). These
two elements are held in tension: neither resolves into the other. The experience of a large Newman is
neither comfortable pleasure nor simple fear but something more vertiginous.

This combination — the sublime as the terror-inflected beautiful — is what distinguishes Newman's
color fields from purely aestheticizing approaches. The scale is not chosen for decorative effect but
as an instrument of existential confrontation.

## Scale as Spiritual Experience

### Vir Heroicus Sublimis (1950–1951)

Newman's most celebrated work measures **7 feet 11 inches × 17 feet 9.25 inches**. At this scale,
the painting cannot be taken in from a single viewpoint. The viewer must move; peripheral vision is
overwhelmed; the field seems to continue beyond the edges of the canvas, beyond the room itself.

Newman issued specific instructions for viewing: stand close — approximately 18 inches from the
surface. At this distance, the scale becomes absolute and the color fills consciousness. The five
zips (in this case, the bands of slightly different cadmium red shades) become human-scale measures,
and the vast red field becomes the space of the sublime.

Newman insisted that paintings should be experienced up close, not from a distance: "I hope that my
painting has the impact of giving someone, as it did me, the feeling of his own totality, of his own
separateness, of his own individuality."

### The Instructions for Viewing

Newman reportedly gave viewers of Vir Heroicus Sublimis specific instructions: stand close, at
about 18 inches. This instruction reveals the painting's intended function. At normal viewing
distance (8–10 feet), the painting is a beautiful, large, nearly monochromatic canvas with a few
vertical bands. At 18 inches, it is an overwhelming field of color that has no edges within view —
an experience of immersion, of being inside color rather than looking at it.

The physical experience of being close to the surface also reveals the painting's material qualities:
the different textures of the zips (applied with a palette knife), the slight variations in the
field color, the evidence of the painter's hand.

## Color Fields and the Zip as Divider/Creator

### Color Field Properties

Newman's color fields are not undifferentiated flat planes of color. Examined carefully, they show:

- **Directional brushwork**: The paint is applied in one direction, creating a grain or fiber-like
  texture that gives the surface life
- **Subtle value variation**: The field is not absolutely uniform — slight variations in density
  and opacity create a pulsating, breathing quality
- **Edge management**: At the canvas edges, the color is sometimes allowed to extend to the edge
  cleanly or managed with careful masking; the relationship of field to edge is carefully considered

These qualities prevent the fields from reading as mere colored surfaces and give them the presence
of atmospheric space: you seem to look into the color, not at it.

### The Creative Function of the Zip

In paintings with multiple zips, the spatial relationships between zips determine the painting's
entire character. Closely spaced zips create tension and compression; widely spaced zips produce
expansiveness. The color of each zip relative to the field and to other zips creates complex optical
interactions.

In Vir Heroicus Sublimis, the five zips (two white, one cadmium orange, one deep maroon, one light
cadmium red) create intervals within the vast red field that function like musical notes — not melody
but rhythm, the establishment of intervals that give the field its measured, legible character.

## Philosophical Writings

### "The Sublime is Now" (1948)

Published in *Tiger's Eye* No. 6, December 1948. Key passage:

"We are asserting man's natural desire for the exalted, for a concern with our relationship to the
absolute emotions. We do not need the obsolete props of an outmoded and antiquated legend. We are
creating images whose reality is self-evident and which are devoid of the props and crutches that
evoke associations with outmoded images, both sublime and beautiful. We are freeing ourselves of
the impediments of memory, association, nostalgia, legend, myth, or what have you, that have been
the devices of Western European painting. Instead of making cathedrals out of Christ, man, or 'life,'
we are making it out of ourselves, out of our own feelings."

This passage is perhaps the most precise statement of the entire Abstract Expressionist program:
the rejection of European mythological, religious, and historical subject matter in favor of direct
emotional address through abstract means.

## Famous Works

### Vir Heroicus Sublimis, 1950–1951
**Medium**: Oil on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 95 3/8 × 213 1/4 inches (242.2 × 541.7 cm)  
**Location**: Museum of Modern Art, New York

The title means "Man, Heroic and Sublime" in Latin. A vast field of cadmium red with five vertical
zips (bands of slightly different red shades and one maroon). At its actual scale, it is one of the
most physically overwhelming paintings in existence. Newman was the first painter to use scale as
a primary instrument of sublimity.

### Onement I, 1948
**Medium**: Oil and masking tape on canvas  
**Dimensions**: 27 1/4 × 16 1/4 inches  
**Location**: Museum of Modern Art, New York

The founding work of the zip paintings, painted on Newman's 43rd birthday. Small, almost intimate,
it shows the concept in its most essential form: a field of dark Indian red, a single zip of light
cadmium orange applied over masking tape. The title "Onement" — a word Newman may have coined —
suggests unity, wholeness, the togetherness of opposites.

### Who's Afraid of Red, Yellow and Blue (series, 1966–70)
**Medium**: Acrylic on canvas  
**Dimensions**: Various (Who's Afraid IV: 107.9 × 576.2 cm)  
**Location**: Nationalgalerie, Berlin (Who's Afraid III); Städel Museum (Who's Afraid IV)

A late series challenging the art world's skepticism about his use of primary colors. The title
directly addresses the critics who found his primary palette too simple or decorative. Each canvas
is a vast field of red (or yellow or blue) with single or multiple zips. Who's Afraid of Red, Yellow
and Blue III was attacked with a knife by a vandal in 1986, then controversially restored.
